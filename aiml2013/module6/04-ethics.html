Ethics Thread: When the Description Is All You Have

AIML 2013: Module 6 Discussion. Post by Tuesday, May 5 (before class)

The Prompt

This week you generated image captions with a vision-language model and measured how often it described objects that weren't in the image. Some hallucinations were harmless: a caption mentioning a cloud in a sky photo that was actually cloudless. Others were more consequential: describing a person where none existed, or adding objects that changed the meaning of the scene.

For a sighted person, a wrong caption is easy to dismiss — you look at the image and see the error. For a blind person relying on alt text, the caption is the image. A hallucinated object becomes part of their understanding of the world. A fabricated person in a room description, a nonexistent hazard on a sidewalk, or a wrong medication label in a pharmacy photo could all have real consequences.

In your post, address at least one of the following:

  • Your prompt sensitivity test (Cell 7) showed that a "cautious" prompt reduced hallucinations but also reduced detail. If you are building an accessibility tool, you face a tradeoff: a cautious caption protects users from false information but may omit real objects they need to know about. A detailed caption gives a richer description but includes invented objects the user cannot verify. How should a developer navigate this tradeoff? Should the user get to choose?
  • Commercial VLMs like Gemini Vision, GPT-4V, and Claude are already being used to generate alt text at scale. Most users have no way to know whether a given alt text was human-written or AI-generated. Should AI-generated descriptions be labeled? Would the label help or harm users?
  • Your lab used Gemini as both the caption generator and the caption judge (LLM-as-judge in Cell 4). The same model that invented objects was asked to evaluate whether those objects were real. Is this circular evaluation acceptable for a research exercise? Would it be acceptable for a deployed accessibility product? What would a better evaluation pipeline look like?
  • VLMs trained on web-scraped images and captions inherit the biases of that data. If a VLM consistently captions images of women in kitchens but men in offices, it is not just describing — it is reinforcing stereotypes through its descriptions. For a blind user, the model's gender assumptions become the default understanding of the image. Is this a hallucination problem, a bias problem, or both?

You measured how often a VLM invents objects. For most applications, a few hallucinated objects are a nuisance. For accessibility, they are a failure of trust. The ethical question is not whether VLMs are good enough for captioning — they are already being used — but whether the people who depend on the descriptions have any way to know when the description is wrong.
