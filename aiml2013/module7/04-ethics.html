Ethics Thread: What You'd Build Differently

AIML 2013: Module 7 Discussion (Final). Post by Tuesday, May 12 (before class)

The Prompt

This is the last ethics discussion of the semester. Over the past seven weeks, you have built CV systems and asked ethical questions about each one: how edge detectors encode assumptions about what matters in an image, whether visual search enables surveillance, how accuracy gaps map to demographic groups, what happens when a VLM hallucinates objects for someone who can't see the image, and what obligations follow from knowing a model's limitations.

Now you know things you didn't know in Week 1. You understand how HOG encodes edge orientations, how neural embeddings compress visual information into vectors, how precision@k measures retrieval quality, how intersectional analysis reveals hidden failures, and how VLMs generate descriptions from visual features. That technical knowledge changes which ethical questions you can ask and which answers you can give.

In your post, address the following:

Pick one project from this semester. Imagine you are asked to deploy it as a real product — not a class project, but a system that real people will use. What is the most important thing you would change before deployment, and why? Your answer should be informed by both your technical understanding (what the system actually does, how it fails) and your ethical reasoning (who would be affected, what the consequences of failure would be).

You might focus on:

  • An image similarity search engine (Module 4) used by law enforcement to match faces from surveillance cameras.
  • A bias audit pipeline (Module 5) used to certify a facial recognition system for airport security.
  • A captioning system (Module 6) deployed as an accessibility tool for blind users.
  • Or any other deployment scenario that makes the stakes concrete.

The best posts will connect a specific technical limitation you discovered in your lab to a specific ethical consequence in the deployment scenario. General statements about "AI being biased" won't cut it — you've spent a semester going deeper than that.

Requirements
  • Your post: 150–250 words. Take a position. Be specific about which project and which deployment scenario.
  • Reply to a classmate: At least one substantive reply. This is the last one — make it count.
  • Deadline: Initial post by Tuesday, May 12 (before class). Reply by Thursday, May 14.

You started this course knowing that AI has ethical implications. You're ending it knowing exactly what those implications look like inside the systems you built. That specificity is what this course was for.
