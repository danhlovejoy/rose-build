Ethics Thread: What ImageNet Thinks the World Looks Like

AIML 2013: Module 3 Discussion. Post by Tuesday, April 14 (before class)

The Prompt

The embeddings you extracted this week came from MobileNetV2, a network trained on ImageNet: 1.2 million labeled images across 1,000 categories. The network learned to place visually similar images close together in 1,280-dimensional space because doing so helped it predict the correct label. You didn't choose those 1.2 million images. Neither did most of the people whose images they contain.

ImageNet was assembled primarily from internet image searches in the early 2010s. The images it contains, the categories it defines, and the people who labeled them all reflect a particular slice of the world: mostly English-speaking, mostly Western, built from what was findable on the web at the time. When you use MobileNetV2 embeddings to compare images, you're using a definition of "similarity" that came from that slice.

In your post, address at least one of the following:

  • ImageNet's 1,000 categories include fine-grained distinctions between 120 dog breeds but far fewer categories for foods, clothing, and everyday objects from non-Western cultures. When MobileNetV2 embeddings are used in a product recommendation system or a content moderation filter, what populations are systematically underserved by that category structure?
  • In 2019, researchers showed that ImageNet contained images labeled with offensive slurs applied to people in specific occupations and demographic groups. The labels were eventually removed, but the network had already been trained on them. The weights you are using may carry traces of those labels. How should a practitioner think about the legacy of a dataset they can no longer fully inspect?
  • Transfer learning assumes the source task (ImageNet classification) and the target task (your images) are similar enough that the learned features transfer. Your dataset is CIFAR-10. Consider a deployment context where the target dataset is satellite imagery, medical scans, or images from a specific geographic region. When does the "similarity" assumption break down, and what are the consequences?
  • Your lab gives you a concrete comparison: HOG retrieval vs. MobileNetV2 retrieval. HOG is a deterministic formula you can inspect and explain. MobileNetV2 is a black box trained on a dataset you didn't collect. For a high-stakes application (medical triage, criminal evidence, hiring), which would you prefer to rely on, and why?
