Module 3: Image Embeddings

Readings & Prep: Complete before Tuesday, April 14

Estimated prep time: ~1.5 hours (required materials). Two optional sources for those who want to go further. All materials are free.

The big picture: HOG computed a fixed recipe: divide the image into a grid, measure gradient orientations in each cell, concatenate the histograms. Any two engineers given the same image and the same parameters will get the same vector. That determinism is also its limitation: HOG can only encode what the formula was designed to find.

A convolutional neural network trained on 1.2 million labeled images learned its own recipe. Nobody told it to look for edges, then textures, then object parts: it discovered that hierarchy on its own, because that hierarchy is what predicts the correct label. The result is a feature extractor that captures information no hand-designed formula can reach: the difference between a dog and a wolf even when the background is similar, or the similarity between two chairs that share no color, texture, or orientation.

This week you strip the classification head off MobileNetV2, run your Module 2 images through what remains, and compare the resulting embeddings directly against HOG. The retrieval accuracy tables and the dual t-SNE plot in Cells 6 and 9 will show you exactly where learned features win (and where they still fail).

3Blue1Brown • Grant Sanderson • YouTube

Before diving into convolutional networks, you need a clear mental model of what a neural network is at all. This video (chapter 1 of 3Blue1Brown’s Neural Networks series) builds that model from scratch. It covers neurons, layers, weights, biases, and activation functions, using handwritten digit recognition as the running example. The visualizations are exceptional. By the end you will understand what it means for a network to “learn” and what the numbers inside it actually represent.

Watch this first. The Computerphile video and the Szeliski reading both assume you already know what a layer and a neuron are. This video is where you get that foundation.

Watch: But what is a neural network? →

Key concepts to lock in: A neuron is a number. A layer is a column of numbers. Each number in the next layer is computed from a weighted sum of the previous layer, passed through an activation function. The weights are what the network “learns.” Everything in the next four resources builds on exactly this.
Neural Network that Changes Everything (Computerphile) Required Video ~13 min

Computerphile • Dr Mike Pound • YouTube

Dr Mike Pound explains what made convolutional neural networks a step change in image classification accuracy, and why the features they learn are fundamentally different from anything you could design by hand. The video covers what a convolutional layer computes, how features become hierarchical as you go deeper, and why the same network that learned to classify cats and cars also turns out to be useful for classifying almost anything else. This is the motivation for the entire lab: what you are extracting from MobileNetV2 is exactly the hierarchy Pound describes.

Watch: Neural Network that Changes Everything →

Key concept to lock in: Early layers of a CNN respond to edges and simple textures. Middle layers respond to shapes and object parts. Deep layers respond to whole objects and semantic categories. When you strip off the final classification layer and use the last pooled feature vector, you are using everything the network learned except “which of my 1,000 classes is this?” That is the 1,280-dimensional vector your lab extracts.
Computer Vision: Algorithms and Applications, §5.4: Deep Convolutional Networks Required Read ~25 min

Richard Szeliski • 2nd Edition (2022) • Free online

The textbook treatment of deep convolutional networks: how convolutional layers work, what pooling does, why ReLU activations matter, and how classification architectures are structured. Section 5.4 covers the key architectural families (AlexNet, VGG, ResNet, MobileNet) with enough detail to understand what MobileNetV2 is doing when you call it in the lab. Focus especially on the subsections on feature hierarchies and global average pooling: those concepts connect directly to Cell 3 of the lab.

Read: Szeliski, Computer Vision (free online) →

What to focus on: The description of global average pooling and why it produces a fixed-length vector regardless of input resolution. This is the mechanism behind pooling='avg' in Cell 1 of the lab: it collapses the spatial dimensions of the final feature map into a single 1,280-value vector per image.

Interactive: What Does the Network Actually See?

Feature Visualization (Distill.pub) Required Interactive ~20 min

Olah, Mordvintsev, Schubert • Distill • 2017

Distill is a peer-reviewed machine learning journal known for exceptionally clear interactive articles. This one answers the question you should have after watching the Computerphile video: what exactly do those early, middle, and deep layers respond to? The article generates images that maximally activate individual neurons, revealing that early neurons respond to edges and colors, middle neurons respond to textures and patterns, and deep neurons respond to object parts (eyes, fur, wheels). The interactive diagrams let you explore individual neurons at each layer depth.

Scroll slowly and interact with the diagrams. This article is the closest thing to actually opening up a neural network and looking inside.

Explore: Feature Visualization →

What to notice: The features that emerge in the deepest layers look like recognizable object parts even though nobody told the network to learn them. This is the hierarchy that makes transfer learning work: those learned features are genuinely useful for describing visual content, not just for classifying ImageNet categories.

Linear Transformations and Matrices (3Blue1Brown) Optional Video ~11 min

3Blue1Brown • Grant Sanderson • Essence of Linear Algebra, Chapter 3 • YouTube

Chapter 3 of the Essence of Linear Algebra series. A neural network layer is, at its core, a linear transformation: it takes a vector in one space, multiplies it by a weight matrix, and produces a vector in another space. This video builds the geometric intuition for what that means: what it looks like to move through different vector spaces, and why a matrix is just a record of where basis vectors land. If the phrase “1,280-dimensional space” feels abstract, this video is the cure.
