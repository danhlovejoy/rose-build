Deliverable 1: Module 2 Lab Lab

Overview

In the readings and in class, you learned that HOG converts raw pixel data into gradient orientation histograms: a fixed-length vector that encodes local edge structure while discarding color and exact pixel positions. This lab puts that idea into practice. You'll compute HOG descriptors on a set of images, build a cosine similarity matrix, retrieve the most similar images for a query, and systematically find where the approach breaks down.

The failures are the point. HOG captures shape and local texture, but it throws away everything else. Finding what it misses tells you exactly why deep learning eventually replaced hand-crafted features for most vision tasks.

Deliverable 2: Module 2 Presentation Presentation

Overview

A three- to five-minute presentation during Tuesday's class (April 7, 7:00 PM) about what you learned building the HOG retrieval system. This is not a formal slide deck; think of it as a short show-and-tell for your classmates.

What to Cover

In three to five minutes, walk us through:

  1. The retrieval: Show one query image and its top matches by HOG similarity. Did the system find visually similar images?
  2. The descriptor: Show the HOG visualization overlay for one image and explain what the gradient orientations represent in terms of the image's structure.
  3. The failure: Show a retrieval result where HOG similarity was high but the images look different (or vice versa). Why did the descriptor get it wrong?
  4. Your takeaway: What does HOG capture about an image, and what does it throw away? How does that connect to why deep learning eventually replaced hand-crafted features?

Format

You can share your screen and walk through your notebook, use a few slides, or just talk: whatever communicates your points clearly in three to five minutes. If you attend via Zoom, be prepared to share your screen.

Tip: Practice timing yourself. Five minutes is shorter than you think. Focus on one or two good insights rather than trying to cover everything.
