Module 2: Hand-Crafted Features

Readings & Prep: Complete before Tuesday, April 7

Estimated prep time: ~2 hours 30 minutes (required materials). One optional source for those who want the original paper. All materials are free.

The big picture: Before deep learning, computer vision ran on features that researchers designed by hand. Raw pixels are a poor representation. Two photos of the same dog look completely different as pixel arrays depending on lighting, angle, and background. Researchers solved this by computing something more structured: edges, their orientations, and how they cluster across local regions of the image.

The Histogram of Oriented Gradients (HOG) is the clearest example of this approach. It converts an image into a vector that encodes gradient orientations across a fixed grid of cells, throwing away color and exact pixel positions, keeping shape and local texture. That vector can be compared to other images using cosine similarity. This week's lab is built on this idea: compute HOG descriptors, compare vectors, and find out exactly where the approach works and where it breaks down.

3Blue1Brown: Dot Products and Duality | Ch. 9, Essence of Linear Algebra Required Video ~14 min

3Blue1Brown (Grant Sanderson) • YouTube

Cosine similarity is the dot product of two unit vectors. This video gives you the geometric intuition: why the dot product measures the alignment of two vectors, and what that alignment means. Watch the first 8 minutes for the core idea. The animation makes the concept click in a way that a formula alone doesn't.

Watch: Dot Products on YouTube →

The key connection: A HOG descriptor is a vector. Two images with similar HOG vectors have a high cosine similarity score. "Similar vectors" means "similar gradient distributions" means "similar visual structure." That chain is the entire logic of the lab's retrieval system.

Discussion questions for class:
  1. Raw pixel values and HOG descriptors are both representations of the same image. What information does HOG preserve that raw pixels don't, and what does it discard?
  2. HOG divides the image into a fixed grid of cells and bins gradient orientations within each cell. What do you expect to happen to retrieval accuracy if you make cells much larger? Much smaller?
  3. Cosine similarity measures the angle between two vectors, not their magnitude. Why is angle a better measure than Euclidean distance for comparing HOG descriptors?
  4. HOG was state-of-the-art for pedestrian detection in 2005. By 2012 it was largely superseded by deep networks. What problem does a CNN solve that HOG cannot?
Bridge to NLP (AIML 2003): NLP students this week are building TF-IDF vectors — another form of explicit feature engineering that transforms raw text into a fixed-length numerical vector before any classification. HOG does for images what TF-IDF does for text: both discard irrelevant variation (pixel noise, common words), keep meaningful structure (gradient orientations, distinctive word distributions), and produce vectors that enable comparison via cosine similarity. In Week 4, both courses replace these hand-crafted representations with learned embeddings.
