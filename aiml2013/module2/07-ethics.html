Ethics Thread: Humans as Objects to Be Detected

AIML 2013: Module 2 Discussion. Post by Tuesday, April 7 (before class)

The Prompt

The HOG descriptor you built this week was originally developed by Dalal and Triggs in 2005 for a specific task: pedestrian detection. The algorithm reduces a person's silhouette to a pattern of gradient orientations that a classifier can match against a template. Within a few years, this technology moved from a conference paper to deployed surveillance systems in public spaces, train stations, and traffic cameras.

The jump from "detect a shape" to "track a person" is a short one. HOG doesn't know what a pedestrian is; it knows what a pedestrian's gradient pattern looks like. But the systems built on top of it, the ones that decide when to alert, when to record, when to flag, are making decisions about people.

In your post, address at least one of the following:

  • HOG was designed to detect "pedestrians": a category defined by the researchers and their dataset. The original training data was collected from a specific set of street scenes. What kinds of people might be underrepresented in that data, and what happens when the detector is deployed in a different context?
  • Pedestrian detection is used in self-driving cars, surveillance cameras, and crowd-monitoring systems. Each application carries different stakes. Should the same detection technology require different ethical standards depending on who's using it and why?
  • The Dalal and Triggs paper frames pedestrian detection as an object recognition problem (the "object" being a human body). What changes, ethically, when the object being detected is a person rather than a car or a stop sign?
  • Your lab computed HOG descriptors and used them for image similarity retrieval. The same technique scales to searching for a specific person across thousands of camera feeds. Where is the line between a useful computer vision tool and a surveillance system?
