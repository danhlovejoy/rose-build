Ethics Thread: Whose Images Train the Machine?

AIML 2013: Week 1 Discussion. Post by Tuesday, March 31

The Prompt

The Szeliski reading introduces computer vision as a field that processes images computationally. The Computerphile video shows how images are broken into pixel values. But where do the images that train vision systems come from?

Large datasets like ImageNet contain millions of photographs — many scraped from the internet without the knowledge or consent of the people in them. When a vision model is trained on these datasets, it's learning patterns in data collected without explicit permission.

In your post, address at least one of the following:

  • When a vision model is trained on images scraped from the internet, whose faces and lives are represented? Whose are missing? What are the consequences?
  • If a computer vision system can identify you in a photograph, who should have the right to use that capability? Should you have a say?
  • Should the people whose photographs appear in training datasets (photographers, subjects) have had a say in how their images were used? Why or why not?
  • You're about to use computer vision tools for class assignments. What's one ethical question you think you should be asking yourself as you do?
Requirements
  • Your post: 150–250 words. Take a position. Don't just summarize the question.
  • Reply to a classmate: At least one substantive reply (not just "I agree"). Engage with their reasoning: push back, build on it, or offer a different angle.
  • Deadline: Initial post by Tuesday, March 31 (before class). Reply by the following Tuesday class.

This is the first of a recurring ethics thread that runs alongside the technical content all semester. Thoughtful answers differ from lazy ones. I'm looking for evidence that you've considered the question, not that you've landed on the "correct" position.
