Week 1: Vision & Images

Readings & Prep: Complete before Tuesday, March 31

Estimated prep time: ~2.5 hours of reading, watching, and experimenting (required materials). The optional sources go deeper; use NotebookLM to help you navigate them. All materials are free.

The big picture: Before a computer can "see" anything, an image must become a grid of numbers. A photograph is not a picture to a machine — it's a multidimensional array of pixel values. This week, we build that foundation: how images are captured, stored, and represented as data structures that code can manipulate.

Required Readings & Videos

These materials form the core foundation. Work through them in order.

Szeliski, Ch. 1.1–1.2: Introduction to Computer Vision Required Read ~30 min

Computer Vision: Algorithms and Applications, 2nd Edition (Springer Nature)

The foundational overview of what computer vision is, its history, and the core problems the field tries to solve. Szeliski is the standard reference text for the field. This section establishes the context and vocabulary you'll use all semester.

Read: Szeliski Online (free) →

Wolberg: Digital Image Fundamentals Required Read ~20 min

George Wolberg, City College of New York

Prof. Wolberg's concise overview of image formation, sampling, quantization, and how images are represented digitally. This is the practical complement to Szeliski: where Szeliski shows you the big picture, Wolberg shows you how pixels work under the hood.

Read: Digital Image Fundamentals (PDF) →

3Blue1Brown: Vectors | Ch. 1, Essence of Linear Algebra Required Video ~10 min

3Blue1Brown (Grant Sanderson)

A beautiful visual introduction to what vectors are and why they matter. The foundation for understanding image data as arrays and, later, embeddings. If you've never seen this series, it's one of the best mathematical visualizations ever made.

Watch: Vectors on YouTube →

Computerphile: Digital Images Required Video ~10 min

Computerphile (Mike Pound)

Mike Pound explains how images are represented in a computer: pixels, 32-bit color, and RGBA. A perfect companion to the Wolberg reading. Computerphile videos are known for making technical concepts crystal clear.

Watch: Digital Images on YouTube →

Experiment: Get Your Hands Dirty

These interactive tools let you see the concepts in action. Spend 5–10 minutes playing with each one.

Evident Scientific: Spatial Resolution Explorer Required Interactive ~5 min

Evident Scientific (Olympus)

Drag the slider and watch what happens to the image as resolution changes. This is the Wolberg reading come to life: you're seeing exactly what "sampling" and "quantization" do to visual information. How low can the resolution go before you can no longer recognize the subject?

Experiment: Resolution Explorer →

Setosa.io: Image Kernels Explained Visually Required Interactive ~10 min

Victor Powell & Lewis Lehe • Setosa.io

This interactive visualization lets you select different kernels (blur, sharpen, edge detect) and see exactly what they do to an image, pixel by pixel. You can watch the kernel slide across the image and see how each output pixel is computed. This is the single best way to understand what "convolving a kernel over an image" means before you write any code. Play with every kernel option.

Experiment: Image Kernels Visualizer →

Why this matters: Next week's lab (Edge Detection & Blurring Kernels) is built entirely around these operations. The more intuition you build here, the faster the lab will go.

Computerphile: How Blurs & Filters Work Required Video ~8 min

Computerphile (Mike Pound)

Mike Pound walks through how a kernel slides across an image to produce a blur, and how different kernels produce different effects. This is the intuition you need before writing your first cv2.filter2D() call.

Watch: How Blurs & Filters Work →

Computerphile: Finding the Edges (Sobel Operator) Required Video ~12 min

Computerphile (Mike Pound)

Extends the kernel concept to edge detection. Pound explains how the Sobel operator works: why it uses two kernels (horizontal and vertical), what the gradient means, and why edges are where the interesting information lives in an image.

Watch: Finding the Edges →

  2. The Wolberg reading covers sampling and quantization. What trade-offs are involved in choosing image resolution and bit depth?
  3. How does thinking of images as arrays change the way you think about what a computer "sees"?
Bridge to NLP (AIML 2003): This week, NLP students are learning that text must become numbers (tokens and embeddings) before an LLM can process it. You're learning the same thing for images: pixels are to vision as tokens are to language. Both are transformations from human-readable content into numerical arrays that machines can compute on.
