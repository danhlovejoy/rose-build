Deliverable 1: Lab 1 — Edge Detection & Blurring Kernels Lab

Overview

In the readings and in class, you learned that images are arrays of numbers. Now you'll learn that filters — small matrices called kernels — are how computers extract meaningful features from those arrays. A blurring kernel smooths noise. An edge-detection kernel finds boundaries between objects. These are the building blocks of every vision pipeline, from Instagram filters to self-driving cars.

What to Build

Create a Colab notebook that loads an image and applies a series of filters to it. Your notebook should include:

  1. Load and display an image as a NumPy array. Choose a photograph with clear structure (not a blurry phone selfie). Display it, print its shape and dtype, and show a single-channel (grayscale) version.
  2. Apply at least three blurring filters: box blur, Gaussian blur, and median blur. Use OpenCV (cv2) or equivalent. Display the results side-by-side with the original. In a markdown cell, explain what each kernel is doing mathematically — what does "convolving a kernel over an image" mean?
  3. Apply edge detection: Use a Sobel filter (horizontal and vertical separately), then apply Canny edge detection. Display the results. In a markdown cell, explain why edge detection works — what is the kernel measuring in the pixel values?
  4. Experiment with kernel size. Run the same filter (e.g., Gaussian blur) with at least three different kernel sizes (e.g., 3×3, 7×7, 15×15). Display the results and comment on how kernel size affects the output.
  5. Reflection (200–400 words in a markdown cell at the end): Connect your results to the readings. What did the filters reveal about the image that wasn't obvious to your eye? How does this relate to how machines "see" differently from humans? What would you do differently next time?

What to Submit

  • A completed Colab notebook (.ipynb) saved to your GitHub repo under week2/.
  • All cells executed with visible outputs (images must render in GitHub's notebook viewer).
  • Submit the GitHub link in Canvas.
Start this in Thursday lab. We'll work through loading images and applying your first kernel together. Come with OpenCV installed in your Colab environment and a test image ready.
Don't just run the code — explain it. The rubric weights understanding heavily. If your notebook has code but no markdown cells explaining what's happening and why, you'll lose points on both the Task Requirements and Reflection criteria.

Deliverable 3: Week 2 Presentation Presentation

Overview

A three- to five-minute presentation during Tuesday's class (March 31) about what you learned building the lab. This is not a formal slide deck; think of it as a short show-and-tell for your classmates.

What to Cover

In three to five minutes, walk us through:

  1. The best before-and-after: Show one specific example where a filter revealed something interesting about your image. What did it look like before, and what did the filter expose?
  2. What the kernel is doing: Pick one filter you applied and explain, in plain language, what the kernel is doing to the pixel values. Don't just say "it blurs the image" — explain how.
  3. Your takeaway: Based on your lab results and the readings, how does a computer "see" differently from a human? What surprised you about working with images as arrays?

Format

You can share your screen and walk through your notebook, use a few slides, or just talk: whatever communicates your points clearly in three to five minutes. If you attend via Zoom, be prepared to share your screen.

Tip: Practice timing yourself. Five minutes is shorter than you think. Focus on one or two good insights rather than trying to cover everything.
