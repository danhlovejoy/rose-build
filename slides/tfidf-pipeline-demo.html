Tokenization & Stopword Removal

First, split the text into individual words (tokens). Then remove stopwords — common words like "the," "is," and "a" that appear in every review regardless of sentiment. They carry no signal, so we drop them.

3

TF-IDF Vectorization

Each surviving token gets a TF-IDF score. TF measures how often the word appears in this review. IDF measures how rare it is across all 2,000 reviews. Multiply them together: words that are frequent here but rare elsewhere get the highest scores.

4

The Learned Coefficients

During training, the model read 1,600 labeled reviews and learned which words tend to appear in positive reviews (green) and which tend to appear in negative reviews (red). These coefficients are fixed after training — they don't change when a new review comes in.

The intercept (bias) is −0.05

With no words at all, the model is almost exactly neutral. Every word's contribution pushes the score up or down from this starting point.

Surprise words: "horror" has a slightly negative coefficient — not because horror is bad, but because horror reviews in the training data skewed negative. The model learned statistical associations, not meaning.
↓
5

The Decision: Multiply and Add

For each word in the review, multiply its TF-IDF score by its learned coefficient. Positive products pull toward "positive." Negative products pull toward "negative." The sum of all products (plus the intercept) is the raw score.

6

Sigmoid → Probability → Classification

The raw score can be any number. The sigmoid function squishes it into a value between 0 and 1 — a probability. Above 0.5 means positive. Below 0.5 means negative.
