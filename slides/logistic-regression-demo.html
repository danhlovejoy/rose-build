Logistic Regression: The Binary Classifier

AIML 2003 — Interactive Walkthrough

How does a model learn to draw a line between positive and negative?

1

The Setup

You have a pile of movie reviews. Each one is labeled positive or negative. You want a model that can classify new, unlabeled reviews. That's binary classification — two categories, one decision.

The TF-IDF pipeline already turned each review into a vector of numbers. Now we need a rule that takes those numbers and outputs a class. Logistic regression is that rule.

To see how it works, we'll start with just two features — the TF-IDF scores for two words. That way we can draw the data on a 2D chart and watch the classifier learn.

From Score to Probability

The raw score can be any number — positive, negative, huge, tiny. The sigmoid function squishes it into a value between 0 and 1. That's the model's confidence that the review is positive.

How the Model Learns

The model starts with random weights. It looks at the training data, sees how many reviews it gets wrong, and nudges the weights to reduce errors. Each nudge is small. After many rounds (called iterations), the boundary settles into the position that classifies the most reviews correctly.

5

From 2 Words to 5,000

The demo above uses two words. Your lab used 5,000. The math is identical: multiply each TF-IDF score by its weight, add them up, add the bias, pass through the sigmoid. The only difference is the number of terms in the sum.

raw score = w1×x1 + w2×x2 + … + w5000×x5000 + b

With two features, the decision boundary is a line. With three features, it's a flat plane. With 5,000 features, it's a hyperplane — a flat surface in 5,000-dimensional space. You can't visualize 5,000 dimensions, but the model doesn't need to. It's just doing multiplication and addition, same as the two-word version.

"Still linear" is the key limitation. Logistic regression draws a flat boundary. It can't learn "this word is positive unless that other word is also present." Every word contributes independently. That's why it works well on sentiment (where individual words carry strong signal) but struggles with sarcasm, negation, and context-dependent meaning.

6

Confidence and Failures

The model doesn't just say "positive" or "negative." It says how sure it is. Reviews far from the boundary get high-confidence predictions. Reviews near the boundary are coin flips. The interesting cases are high-confidence mistakes — reviews the model was sure about and still got wrong.

High-confidence failures are the most informative errors. In your lab, Cell 6 finds these cases. They reveal what the model can't see: sarcasm ("a brilliant waste of talent"), negation ("not awful, just boring"), and context that a bag-of-words model misses entirely.
