What Do Embeddings Encode?

From Attributes to Analogy Math

Every word becomes a list of numbers. Those numbers encode meaning. Here's how.

Imagine you could describe any word with a handful of measurable attributes. Each attribute gets a number from 0 to 1. The result is a vector — a list of numbers that encodes what the word means.

Zero Values: Not Every Attribute Applies

Royalty, gender, and age are meaningful for people — but not for everything. What happens when a word has no connection to an attribute? That dimension goes to zero. The vector is still the same length, but some slots are empty.

This is what "sparse" means. When most values in a vector are zero, we call it sparse. TF-IDF vectors are extremely sparse: thousands of dimensions, almost all zero. Embedding vectors are dense: hundreds of dimensions, almost none zero. The zeros here are the conceptual bridge — they show why sparse representations have limits.

From Toy Vectors to Real Embeddings

Our "toy" model above has 8 dimensions with human-readable labels. A real embedding model like all-MiniLM-L6-v2 produces vectors with 384 dimensions. No dimension has a name. No single number means "royalty" or "gender." The meaning is distributed across all 384 values.

Real embeddings are dense. In our toy model, “Water” has 6 zero dimensions out of 8 (75% sparse). In the real all-MiniLM-L6-v2 vector for “water,” virtually every dimension carries a nonzero value. That density is what lets embeddings capture subtle relationships that sparse representations miss. The trade-off: you can no longer point to one dimension and say “that’s the royalty dimension.” The meaning is distributed across all 384 numbers.
Negative values are normal. Our toy vectors use 0 to 1 because each dimension has a human-readable label — “Royalty” is either present or it isn’t. The real vectors above range from about −0.17 to 0.25. No dimension has a name, so there is no intuitive meaning for “negative Royalty.” Instead, positive and negative values together define a direction in high-dimensional space, and it is the direction that encodes meaning.
The analogy still works

The same King − Man + Woman = Queen arithmetic works in 384 dimensions. Mikolov et al. showed this in 2013 with Word2Vec. The relationships are harder to visualize, but the geometry is identical: meaning is direction, and analogy is vector subtraction and addition.

4

How Do Words Get Their Vectors?

Nobody sat down and decided that dimension 47 of “king” should be 0.0476. The numbers are learned by a neural network that reads enormous amounts of text.

The core idea is simple: words that appear in similar contexts should get similar vectors. A network that reads billions of sentences notices that “king” and “queen” show up near the same surrounding words (“throne,” “crown,” “ruled”), so it gradually pulls their vectors closer together. “Water” appears in completely different contexts (“drink,” “river,” “flow”), so its vector drifts to a different region of the space.

The all-MiniLM-L6-v2 model you will use in the lab was trained on over one billion sentence pairs. The model card on Hugging Face (one of this week’s readings) lists the exact training datasets. After training, the 384 numbers for each word are fixed: you load the model, hand it a word or sentence, and it returns the vector. That is what you will do in Cell 1 of the lab.

Go deeper in the readings. The StatQuest video walks through how the training loop works step by step. The Alammar article makes the same process visual with diagrams showing how similar words get pulled together and dissimilar words get pushed apart. Both are assigned this week.
