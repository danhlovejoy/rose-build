Cosine Similarity

Measuring How Alike Two Things Are

The same math works for text and images. Two vectors, one angle, one number.

The Geometry: Two Arrows, One Angle

Forget text and images for a moment. Start with two arrows (vectors) pointing out from the origin. Cosine similarity measures the angle between them. Same direction = 1. Perpendicular = 0. Opposite = −1.

Length doesn't matter. Cosine similarity only cares about direction. A short arrow and a long arrow pointing the same way have similarity 1.0. That's why it works for comparing documents of different lengths or images of different sizes: the magnitude of the vector washes out.
The scale

1.0 = identical direction (perfect match)  •  0.0 = perpendicular (no relationship)  •  −1.0 = opposite direction (as different as possible)

In practice, TF-IDF and HOG vectors are never negative, so cosine similarity for those applications stays between 0 and 1.

Text: Comparing Documents by Their Words

Each document becomes a TF-IDF vector (one number per word in the vocabulary). Two documents that use similar words in similar proportions will point in similar directions. Cosine similarity measures that alignment.

Why cosine instead of counting shared words? Two reviews might share the words "film" and "movie," but those appear in every review and carry low TF-IDF scores. Cosine similarity weights each shared word by how distinctive it is. Sharing the word "masterpiece" matters more than sharing the word "the."

Images: Comparing Pictures by Their Edges

HOG converts an image into a vector of gradient orientation counts: a fixed-length fingerprint of the image's edge structure. Two images with similar HOG vectors have similar shapes and textures, regardless of color or exact pixel values.

The Similarity Matrix

When you have many items (documents or images), you compute cosine similarity for every pair and arrange the results in a matrix. Each cell shows how similar two items are. The diagonal is always 1.0 (every item is identical to itself).

Bridge: NLP ↔ CV. The matrix looks the same whether you're comparing movie reviews or photographs. In your NLP lab, the similarity matrix finds reviews with overlapping vocabulary. In your CV lab, it finds images with overlapping edge structure. The retrieval logic is identical: sort the row by similarity, return the top matches.

When Cosine Similarity Fails

Same meaning, different words (NLP)

"The movie was excellent" and "The film was outstanding" mean the same thing, but share zero distinctive words after stopword removal. Cosine similarity on TF-IDF vectors: 0.0. The vectors point in completely different directions because the features (individual words) don't overlap.

Same shape, different context (CV)

A bicycle wheel and a clock face produce similar HOG descriptors: both are circular with radial gradient patterns. High cosine similarity, but the images are semantically unrelated. HOG sees edges, not objects.

The fix for both: learned representations

Word embeddings (NLP) and CNN features (CV) solve this by learning that "excellent" and "outstanding" are similar, or that wheels and clocks are different despite similar edges. That's Week 4. Cosine similarity stays the same. The vectors get smarter.

The metric is not the bottleneck. The representation is. Cosine similarity computes the angle between two vectors and has no opinion about what those numbers encode. TF-IDF vectors for “excellent” and “outstanding” sit far apart in vocabulary space because the words don't overlap, so the metric faithfully reports low similarity. The representation was the wrong tool for the question, not the math. HOG has the same problem with semantics: a bicycle wheel and a clock both produce gradient histograms dominated by radial patterns, so their vectors sit close together in HOG space. Cosine similarity will say they're alike. Given the features it was handed, it's right.
