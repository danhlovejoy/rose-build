Attention: How Context Changes Meaning

AIML 2003 • Interactive Walkthrough

The same word gets a different vector depending on the words around it.

Quick review: dot products

A dot product multiplies two vectors element-by-element and sums the results. If two vectors share high values in the same dimensions, the dot product is large. If they have nothing in common, it’s near zero.

dot([0.9, 0.0, 0.3], [0.3, 0.5, 0.7]) = (0.9 × 0.3) + (0.0 × 0.5) + (0.3 × 0.7) = 0.27 + 0 + 0.21 = 0.48

You used dot products in the cosine similarity demo. Cosine similarity normalizes by vector length; the raw dot product does not. Attention uses raw dot products because the magnitude carries useful information — longer vectors (stronger signals) should contribute more.

One Word, Two Meanings

Word2Vec and similar models assign one vector per word. “Bank” gets the same list of numbers whether it appears next to “river” or “account.” That’s a problem. The two meanings share a spelling but almost nothing else.

Two sentences, same word

“The river bank was steep.”

“The bank account was empty.”

Here is the single static vector for “bank” in our toy space. It scores moderately on both Water and Money because the vector tries to cover both meanings at once. It commits to neither.

One vector cannot represent two meanings. A system that encodes “bank” the same way regardless of context will confuse river banks with financial institutions. Something needs to look at the surrounding words and adjust.

2

Which Words Matter?

The fix: before producing a final vector for “bank,” look at every other word in the sentence and ask how relevant it is. You already know how to measure relevance between two vectors. Take the dot product. A higher dot product means the vectors share more structure, which means the words are more related in this context.

After computing dot products, normalize them with softmax so they sum to 1.0. These normalized scores are the attention weights: how much each word should influence the representation of the word you’re encoding.

Softmax weights always sum to 1.0. Each word distributes its full attention across the sentence. A word that attends strongly to one neighbor necessarily attends less to the others. The attention pattern for “bank” shifts depending on which sentence it appears in — and that shift is what resolves the ambiguity.

The Weighted Mix

Attention weights tell you how relevant each word is. Now use them. Multiply each word’s vector by its attention weight and sum the results. The output is a new vector for “bank” that blends in context from the surrounding words.

context(bank) = w1 × vword1 + w2 × vword2 + w3 × vword3

Now compare. The same word “bank” produces two different context vectors:

Context resolved the ambiguity. In the nature sentence, the context vector for “bank” scores high on Water and low on Money. In the finance sentence, the opposite. The surrounding words pulled the representation in different directions. That pull is attention.

Preview: Query, Key, Value

In Stages 2 and 3, we used the raw word vectors for everything: to compute relevance scores and to mix into the output. Real transformers don’t do that. They learn three separate projections of each word vector, each with a different job.

Query (Q)

“What am I looking for?”

Used by the word that’s asking for context.

Key (K)

“What do I contain?”

Used by each candidate word to advertise its content.

Value (V)

“What do I pass along?”

The actual information that gets mixed into the output.

In our demo, the same vector played all three roles. A real transformer multiplies each word vector by three learned weight matrices to produce Q, K, and V separately. The attention score becomes dot(Q, K) instead of dot(raw, raw), and the weighted sum uses V instead of the raw vectors.

This separation is what makes attention trainable. The model can learn to search for one pattern (Q), advertise a different pattern (K), and pass along a third (V). Our toy demo used fixed vectors and got reasonable results; learned projections are what make the mechanism powerful enough for language.

What changes with Q, K, V?

The core operation stays the same: dot products to measure relevance, softmax to normalize, weighted sum to mix. The difference is that the vectors fed into each step are specialized rather than shared. That specialization is learned during training on billions of sentences. The full mechanics are a separate demo.
