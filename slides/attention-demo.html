The Problem: Long-Distance Relationships

Read this sentence: “The trophy didn’t fit in the suitcase because it was too big.”

What does “it” refer to — the trophy or the suitcase? You resolve this by scanning backward through the sentence. Toggle the last word and the referent flips.

—
“it” → trophy
—
“it” → suitcase
Early models had no mechanism for this. They processed words sequentially, passing a fixed-size state vector forward. By the time a pronoun appeared 8 tokens later, the noun it referred to had often decayed out of that state.

3

Three Roles Every Word Plays

Each word gets multiplied by three learned weight matrices, producing three vectors with different jobs. Click each card to see what the role does.

Query (Q)

“What am I looking for?”

Key (K)

“What do I advertise?”

Value (V)

“What do I pass along?”

The word’s question. For “it,” the query encodes something like “I’m a pronoun — which noun do I refer to?” It gets compared against every key to produce scores.

The word’s label. “Trophy” broadcasts a key encoding “singular noun, physical object, potential referent.” A high dot product between a query and this key produces a high attention score.

The payload. Once a word wins a high score, its value is what actually gets blended into the output. Values carry the semantic content forward; queries and keys are just the routing mechanism.

attention(Q, K, V) = softmax(Q · KT) × V
Query meets all keys; the best-matching keys let their values through. Because Q, K, and V are separate projections, the model can learn to search for one pattern (Q), advertise another (K), and transmit a third (V).

The Arithmetic: From Scores to Weights

Pick a word. Its Query is dot-producted against the Key of every word, producing raw scores. Softmax converts those scores into percentages that sum to 100%. The percentages control how much each Value contributes to the output.

5

The Full Pipeline

Transformers stack dozens of attention layers and run multiple heads per layer. Here is what one layer does, end to end.

1

Project into Q, K, V

Each word’s embedding (a single vector) gets multiplied by three separate weight matrices — WQ, WK, WV — producing three specialized vectors. The model learns these matrices during training. A word like “it” ends up with a Query that encodes “I need a noun referent,” while “trophy” gets a Key that says “I am a referent.”

2

Compute compatibility scores

The current word’s Query is dot-producted with every Key in the sentence, including its own. High dot product = strong match. For “it” querying “trophy,” the score is high because the pronoun’s query aligns with the noun’s key.

3

Normalize with softmax

Softmax exponentiates each score and divides by the total, producing weights that are all positive and sum to 1.0. The highest score gets the largest share; the lowest gets a sliver but never zero.

4

Weighted sum of Values

Each Value is scaled by its softmax weight and the results are summed. The output for “it” is a vector that’s ~52% trophy, ~15% suitcase, and small fractions of everything else.

5

Repeat across heads and layers

8–64 heads run in parallel per layer, each tracking a different relationship type (coreference, syntax, proximity, etc.). Their outputs concatenate and feed into the next layer. GPT-4 class models stack 80+ layers. Stage 6 below shows what four heads look like on this sentence.

Parallelism is why this scales. Sequential models processed one word at a time; attention processes every pair at once. That tradeoff — more compute per step, but far fewer steps — is what made training on the entire internet feasible.
