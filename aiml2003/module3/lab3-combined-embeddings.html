The Task

In Module 2, both courses built hand-crafted feature vectors: TF-IDF for text, HOG for images. Both were fixed recipes that encode local patterns and discard context. This week both courses replace those hand-crafted features with learned embeddings from pre-trained neural networks.

This combined lab puts both pipelines in one notebook. You will generate text embeddings with a sentence transformer and image embeddings with MobileNetV2, build retrieval systems for each, and then compare the two vector spaces side by side. The comparison is the point: text and images are fundamentally different kinds of data, yet both can be compressed into dense vectors where cosine similarity measures "closeness." What does "close" mean in each space? Where does the analogy hold, and where does it break?

Part 4: Comparing Vector Spaces

# Cell 8: Dimensionality and structure

What it does: Computes and prints a detailed side-by-side comparison of the text and image embedding spaces: their shape, numeric ranges, sparsity, and clustering quality. Measures how well each space separates same-class items from different-class items.

Takes in: text_embeddings (60×384 array from Cell 3), text_categories (list from Cell 2), text_cat_names (list from Cell 2), image_embeddings (n_images×1280 array from Cell 6), img_labels (list from Cell 5), img_names (list from Cell 5).

Must produce:

  • For each embedding space (text and image):
    • Compute the full pairwise cosine similarity matrix: Call cosine_similarity(embeddings, embeddings) to get a matrix of shape (n, n) where (i, j) is the cosine similarity between items i and j.
    • Separate intra-class and inter-class pairs: Use boolean masking. For each row i and column j:
      • intra_class: pairs where i ≠ j but label[i] == label[j] (same class, different items).
      • inter_class: pairs where label[i] != label[j] (different classes).
    • Compute statistics:
      • Embedding dimension: 384 for text, 1,280 for images.
      • Value range: Min and max of the entire embedding matrix (using np.min() and np.max()).
      • Sparsity: Percentage of exact zeros (same calculation as Cell 6).
      • Mean intra-class cosine similarity: Average of similarity[intra_class].
      • Mean inter-class cosine similarity: Average of similarity[inter_class].
      • Separation gap: Mean intra-class minus mean inter-class. A larger gap means the space clusters same-class items tightly and spreads different-class items apart.
  • Print a formatted table or side-by-side comparison: Show all six metrics for text and images in columns. Example format:
    Metric | Text Embeddings | Image Embeddings
    Dimension | 384 | 1,280
    [etc.]

Notes: The separation gap is a key metric for clustering quality. For text, "class" means the four categories (technology, sports, cooking, travel). For images, "class" means the CIFAR-10 classes (airplane, automobile, etc.). A larger gap in one modality suggests that embeddings for that modality cluster more cleanly by meaning. Compare the gaps: does text separate better than images, or vice versa? This reflects the relative difficulty of organizing each modality.
