Module 3: Text Embeddings

Readings & Prep: Complete before Tuesday, April 14

Estimated prep time: ~1.5 hours (required materials). One optional source for those who want the full Google ML Crash Course treatment. All materials are free.

The big picture: TF-IDF gave each word its own dimension. “Excellent” and “outstanding” are completely unrelated in that space (different columns, zero overlap) even though they mean the same thing. A model built on TF-IDF can only know that two texts are similar if they share vocabulary.

Embeddings fix this by throwing out the dictionary. Instead of one dimension per word, you get a fixed-length vector of real numbers where meaning lives in geometry: texts that mean similar things land near each other, regardless of the words they use. “The food was delicious” and “The meal tasted amazing” will be close neighbors. “The food was delicious” and “The quarterly earnings report” will not.

This week you will load a pre-trained sentence transformer, encode a curated dataset into embedding vectors, build a semantic search engine, and compare it directly against the TF-IDF retrieval you built in Module 2. The comparison is the point: same data, same cosine similarity, completely different geometry.

Watch: Word Embedding and Word2Vec →

Key concept to lock in: An embedding is a lookup table that assigns each word a point in space. The training process positions similar words near each other. After training, you can do arithmetic: vector(“king”) − vector(“man”) + vector(“woman”) ≈ vector(“queen”). This is not a coincidence: it falls out of how the vectors were trained.

all-MiniLM-L6-v2 Model Card Required Read ~10 min

Hugging Face • Model Hub

This is the model card for all-MiniLM-L6-v2, the exact sentence transformer you will use in the lab. A model card is documentation that ships with a pre-trained model: what it was trained on, what it is good at, how to use it, and where it falls short. Reading model cards is a core skill in applied ML. You would not use a library without reading its docs; you should not use a model without reading its card.

Pay attention to the “Usage” section (you will recognize the code from the quickstart), the training data list (notice the scale and variety), and the evaluation results table (the model is ranked against alternatives on specific benchmarks). You do not need to understand every metric, but you should leave with a sense of how practitioners choose one model over another.

Interactive: See It Before You Build It

Google Embedding Projector Required Interactive ~15 min

Google • projector.tensorflow.org

An interactive 3D visualization of real word embeddings. The default dataset shows 10,000 words from a Word2Vec model trained on Google News. You can search for any word and watch its nearest neighbors light up in the embedding space. Try: search for “cat” and see what lands nearby. Then search for “king” and compare. Switch between the PCA and t-SNE projections: note how the same vectors look different depending on the dimensionality reduction method. This is the embedding space you will build for text in Cell 4 of the lab, but pre-made and interactive.

Explore: Google Embedding Projector →

What to notice: Countries cluster together. Sports terms cluster together. Abstract concepts cluster in their own region. The clusters are not programmed: they emerge from training on co-occurrence patterns in text. Your lab’s t-SNE in Cell 9 is a 2D version of exactly what you are looking at here.
