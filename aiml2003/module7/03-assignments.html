Deliverable 1: Final Portfolio Presentation

Overview

A five- to seven-minute presentation during the final class session (Tuesday, May 12, 5:30 PM). You showcase 2–3 of your best projects from the semester, explain the technical choices you made, and reflect on what you learned. See the Portfolio Prep Guide for detailed guidance on structure, project selection, and visual aids.

What to Cover

  1. The arc (1 minute): Where you started, where you ended up, and the trajectory between them.
  2. 2–3 projects (3–4 minutes): For each project: what you built (one sentence), what you learned (the insight, not the code), and what you'd change (critical thinking). Show a strong visual for each project.
  3. Reflection (1 minute): The most important thing you learned that isn't a technical skill. Connect your work to a bigger idea about NLP, AI, or technology.

Format

You may present from slides, notebooks, or a combination. Share your screen. If you attend via Zoom, have all materials open and ready before your turn. Presentations happen in the order you sign up on Tuesday May 5.

What to Submit

  • The presentation itself is delivered live — no file submission required.
  • If you use slides, upload them to your GitHub repo at portfolio/ for reference.
  • Your GitHub repo should be complete and clean: all lab notebooks in their week folders, README updated, no exposed secrets.

Deliverable 2: Final Reflection

Overview

A written reflection submitted in Canvas. Due Thursday, May 14 by 11:59 PM (finals week). This is separate from the portfolio presentation — it's a longer, more thoughtful piece that you write after the presentations are done.

Prompt

Write a 500–800 word essay addressing the following:

  1. The technical arc: Which NLP concepts were hardest to understand at the start of the semester? Which clicked during a specific project? Point to a specific moment (a lab cell, a debugging session, a class discussion) where something shifted in your understanding.
  2. The tool question: You used GenAI tools (Gemini, Claude, ChatGPT) to help you write code all semester. How did your use of these tools change from Week 1 to Week 8? Are you a better programmer because of them, a worse one, or something more complicated?
  3. The ethics thread: You wrote an ethics post every week. Which ethical question from the semester stuck with you? Has your position on it changed since you first wrote about it?
  4. Looking ahead: If you were to build one more project using what you've learned, what would it be? Why?

Requirements

  • 500–800 words. Quality over quantity.
  • Specific examples. Don't write in generalities — point to particular projects, cells, readings, and discussions.
  • Honest. The reflection is graded on thoughtfulness, not on having the "right" answer. If you found something frustrating, say so and explain why.

Where to Submit

Submit in Canvas as a text entry or file upload. This is a Canvas Assignment in the Final Reflection assignment group (5% of your final grade).

Taking AIML 2013 too?

Dual-enrolled students write a single "Synthesis Reflection" (600–1000 words) submitted to both courses. In addition to the prompts above, address: What are the mathematical commonalities between NLP and CV? (Vectors, similarity, optimization, representation learning.) Where did the two fields diverge in ways that surprised you?
