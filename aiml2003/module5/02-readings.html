Module 5: Evaluating LLMs

Readings & Prep: Complete before Tuesday, April 28

Estimated prep time: ~1.5 hours (required materials). One optional deep dive on red-teaming. All materials are free.

The big picture: In Module 4 you built a RAG pipeline that relied on Gemini to generate answers grounded in retrieved context. You tested whether it hallucinated on unanswerable questions. That was one test, on one failure mode, designed by you. This week you formalize that instinct into a structured evaluation.

Evaluating an LLM is not like evaluating a classifier. A classifier's outputs are discrete labels — you compare predicted labels to ground truth and compute precision, recall, F1. An LLM's outputs are open-ended text. There is no single correct answer to "Summarize this article" or "Explain photosynthesis." So evaluation requires judgement: you decide what to test, how to score the response, and what counts as a passing grade. That makes the evaluator's design choices as important as the model's performance.

This week's readings cover three things: how LLMs hallucinate (and why it's structural, not accidental), how researchers measure bias in text generation, and the "LLM-as-judge" technique you'll use in the lab to automate scoring.

A Survey on Hallucination in Large Language Models Required Video ~20 min

Yannic Kilcher • YouTube

Yannic Kilcher walks through the hallucination taxonomy from the Huang et al. survey paper. The key distinction is between intrinsic hallucinations (the model contradicts its own source material) and extrinsic hallucinations (the model adds information that cannot be verified from the source). Your RAG hallucination test in Module 4 caught extrinsic hallucinations — the model fabricated answers when no source was available. This video explains the full taxonomy and why hallucination is a structural property of how LLMs generate text, not a bug that can be patched.

Watch: Survey on Hallucination in LLMs →

Key concept to lock in: LLMs generate text by predicting the next token based on probability. They have no mechanism to verify whether a statement is true — they only know whether it is likely given the preceding tokens. Hallucination is not the model making a mistake; it is the model doing exactly what it was trained to do in a context where probability and truth diverge.
Bias and Fairness in Large Language Models: A Survey Required Read ~25 min

Gallegos et al., 2024 • arXiv

Read Sections 1–3 and skim the evaluation methods in Section 5. This survey categorizes the types of bias that appear in language models: gender, race, religion, and intersectional biases. Section 3 explains how biases enter through training data, and Section 5 covers how researchers measure them. The profession-gender association test in your lab (Cell 5) is a simplified version of the techniques described in Section 5.2. You don't need to read the full paper — the first 12 pages and the evaluation methods section give you what you need.

Read: Bias and Fairness in LLMs →

Key concept to lock in: Bias in LLMs is not a single thing. Gender bias manifests differently from racial bias, and both manifest differently depending on the task (generation vs. classification vs. question answering). A model can show no gender bias on one benchmark and significant bias on another. That is why evaluation design matters — the test you build determines what you find.
Judging LLM-as-a-Judge with MT-Bench and Chatbot Arena Required Read ~20 min

Zheng et al., 2023 • arXiv

Read the abstract, introduction, and Section 3 (LLM-as-judge). This paper introduced the idea that you can use one LLM to evaluate another's output — the same technique you'll use in Cell 3 of the lab to score Gemini's factual answers. The paper shows that LLM judges agree with human judges about 80% of the time, which is roughly the same rate at which two human judges agree with each other. It also identifies the risks: position bias (the judge prefers the first answer), verbosity bias (the judge prefers longer answers), and self-enhancement bias (the judge rates its own model's outputs higher).

Read: Judging LLM-as-a-Judge →

Key concept to lock in: Using an LLM to judge an LLM is circular — the judge may share the same blind spots as the model being tested. But human evaluation doesn't scale, and automated metrics like BLEU and ROUGE miss semantic meaning. LLM-as-judge is a practical compromise, not a gold standard. You should know its failure modes as well as its strengths.

Red Teaming Language Models to Reduce Harms Optional Read ~30 min

Anthropic's red-teaming paper describes how human testers systematically probe a language model to find harmful outputs. The adversarial prompts they design are more creative than the structured evaluations in your lab, but the underlying logic is the same: you are trying to find the failure modes the model's developers didn't anticipate.
