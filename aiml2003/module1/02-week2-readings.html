The big picture: If you've used ChatGPT, Claude, or Gemini, you've already done prompt engineering: you've written instructions trying to get the model to do what you want. This week, we'll cover the foundations (zero-shot, few-shot, chain-of-thought) but then focus on the core trend of 2025–2026: automated optimization and the shift toward context engineering.

Prompts have shifted from hand-crafted artifacts to automatically optimized programs. Leading AI labs now frame the discipline as "context engineering": designing the optimal configuration of system prompts, tool definitions, examples, retrieved documents, and conversation history within a finite attention budget. Understanding this shift is essential for anyone building with LLMs today.

"Prompting Inversion": You Don't Need Prompt Engineering Anymore Pick 1 Paper ~20 min

Imran Khan • October 2025

Shows that sophisticated prompting strategies that help mid-tier models (GPT-4o) harm frontier models (GPT-5). Constraints that prevent errors in weaker models induce hyper-literalism in stronger ones. Accessible and challenges assumptions about universal best practices.

Rethinking Prompting Strategies in LLM Test-Time Scaling Pick 1 Paper ~25 min

Liu, Li et al. • ACL 2025 Outstanding Paper Award

The most rigorous head-to-head comparison of prompting strategies. Tests 8 strategies across 6 LLMs and 6 benchmarks (288 configurations). Finds that simple CoT with majority voting outperforms complex strategies (Tree-of-Thought, Graph-of-Thought) when given more attempts.

Five Themes to Take Away

These ideas connect the readings and frame Week 2 discussion.

1. Automation has overtaken artisanship. Every major advance (GEPA, TextGrad, DSPy, Promptomatix) treats prompt design as an optimization problem with measurable objectives, not a creative writing exercise. The question isn't "how do I phrase this?" but "how do I measure and improve this?"
2. Context engineering has replaced prompt engineering. The challenge is no longer "what words do I use?" but "what information goes into the context window, in what order, at what time?" This architectural perspective is essential for building agents.
3. The optimal strategy depends on the model. The Prompting Inversion finding and the ACL Outstanding Paper both show that what works on one model can hurt another. There is no universally best technique.
4. Reasoning structures have become adaptive. Fixed chain/tree/graph topologies are giving way to dynamic frameworks that allocate reasoning depth based on problem complexity, mirroring how humans think harder about harder problems.
5. Evaluation is now as important as writing prompts. Benchmarks like LangProBe and the ACL scaling study provide rigorous empirical foundations for choosing strategies. Learning to evaluate prompts systematically is now as important as learning to write them.
Discussion questions for class:
  1. What's the difference between "prompt engineering" and "context engineering"? Why does the distinction matter?
  2. The Prompting Inversion paper found that sophisticated prompting can hurt frontier models. What does that imply about how you should approach prompting for a new model?
  3. If automated tools can optimize prompts better than humans, what's the value of learning to write prompts by hand? (Hint: think about what you need to know to use the automated tools effectively.)
  4. The ACL Outstanding Paper found that simple CoT + majority voting beats complex strategies as you give the model more attempts. When might complex strategies still be worth the effort?
  5. Pick one of the five themes above. Do you agree with it? What's a counterargument?
