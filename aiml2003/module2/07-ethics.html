Ethics Thread: Who Decides What's "Positive"?

AIML 2003: Module 2 Discussion. Post by Tuesday, April 7 (before class)

The Prompt

Your lab this week trains a sentiment classifier on the NLTK movie reviews corpus. The corpus contains 2,000 reviews labeled "positive" or "negative" by individual reviewers. When you inspect the TF-IDF model's coefficients, you can see exactly which words push the model toward each category.

Sentiment is not a fact. It's an interpretation. The same word can mean different things in different contexts: "predictable" might be a flaw in a thriller but a virtue in a children's movie. The labels in the training data encode the judgments of the people who wrote them, and the model inherits those judgments as ground truth.

In your post, address at least one of the following:

  • The NLTK movie reviews corpus was labeled by individual reviewers. If those reviewers share similar demographics, tastes, or cultural reference points, what assumptions get baked into the model's definition of "positive" and "negative"?
  • Your lab found words with high positive or negative coefficients. If a word like "predictable" has a negative coefficient, the model treats it as evidence of a bad review, regardless of context. What does this tell you about the limits of training on a single labeled dataset?
  • Sentiment analysis is used at scale: product reviews, social media monitoring, customer feedback scoring. If the training data reflects one community's language patterns, what happens when the model is applied to a different community?
  • You used Gemini to classify the same reviews. Gemini was not trained on this specific dataset, but it carries its own biases from its training data. Is one approach more "fair" than the other, or do they encode different biases?
