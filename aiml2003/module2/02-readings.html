Module 2: Hand-Crafted Features

Readings & Prep: Complete before Tuesday, April 7

Estimated prep time: ~55 minutes (three videos plus three interactive demos). One optional source for those who want the theory behind the formula. All materials are free.

The big picture: Before transformers, text classification was a pipeline of deliberate design choices. Which words should count? How should you handle words that appear in every document? What happens when a word like "bank" means something different in a finance review than in a nature article? TF-IDF — Term Frequency × Inverse Document Frequency — is the canonical answer to the core question: how should a document become a vector?

This week you'll build a complete TF-IDF + logistic regression pipeline on the NLTK movie reviews corpus, visualize exactly which words drive the model's decisions, and then compare it head-to-head against Gemini zero-shot and context-engineered classification on the same 50 test examples. The comparison is the point: a classical pipeline trained in seconds on 2,000 labeled examples, versus a language model that has never been trained on this specific task.

Bag of Words and Text Preprocessing (Video) Required Video ~10 min

Google Cloud Tech • YouTube

Before TF-IDF, text has to go through a pipeline: split into individual words (tokenization), lowercase everything so "The" and "the" count as the same word, remove punctuation, and strip out stop words like "the", "is", and "a" that carry no useful signal. This video walks through each step with examples. Watch it before the TF-IDF video if the concept of a "word count vector" feels unfamiliar, or after if you want to see the preprocessing steps made explicit.

What to notice: After preprocessing, a movie review is no longer a sentence. It is a list of word counts. "This movie was absolutely brilliant!" becomes something like: brilliant=1, movie=1. Every other word was either a stop word or punctuation and gets dropped. TF-IDF then reweights those counts based on how common each word is across all reviews.

Logistic Regression (Video) Required Video ~9 min

StatQuest with Josh Starmer • YouTube

Logistic regression is the classifier your lab trains to predict positive or negative sentiment. This video explains what it does without requiring any calculus. The key ideas: it learns a score for each word (positive score means that word predicts a positive review, negative score means the opposite), it converts that score into a probability between 0 and 1, and the boundary between "positive prediction" and "negative prediction" sits at 0.5. Lab Cell 5 visualizes those word scores directly as a bar chart.

The one concept that matters most: Each word in your vocabulary gets a coefficient. A large positive coefficient for "brilliant" means the model learned that reviews containing "brilliant" tend to be positive. A large negative coefficient for "waste" means the opposite. That is the entire logic behind the bar chart in Cell 5.

Cosine Similarity: Measuring How Alike Two Things Are (Interactive Demo) Required Interactive ~10 min

Dan Lovejoy • AIML 2003 / AIML 2013

Cosine similarity is the metric behind TF-IDF vector comparison. This demo walks through the geometry (Stage 1), then applies it directly to text pairs (Stage 2): type two sentences and watch the score change as shared vocabulary increases or disappears. Stage 4 shows a similarity matrix across six reviews, the same computation used to compare documents in retrieval systems.

What to notice: Stage 5 ("When Cosine Similarity Fails") shows why "excellent" and "outstanding" score 0.0 as TF-IDF vectors despite meaning the same thing. That failure is exactly what motivates the move to sentence embeddings in Module 3.

Logistic Regression: The Binary Classifier (Interactive Demo) Required Interactive ~8 min

Dan Lovejoy • AIML 2003

This demo picks up where the StatQuest video leaves off and applies logistic regression directly to text. Starting from two words and a decision boundary, it builds up to a full 5,000-word vocabulary, showing how the model learns a coefficient for every word and converts a weighted sum into a probability. The confidence and failure section at the end is worth paying attention to before lab.

What to notice: The jump from two words to 5,000 words does not change the math. The model is still multiplying word counts by coefficients and summing them. The vocabulary just gets much longer. Keep that in mind when you look at the coefficient bar chart in Cell 5 of the lab notebook.

Inside the Pipeline: TF-IDF + Logistic Regression (Interactive Demo) Required Interactive ~8 min

Dan Lovejoy • AIML 2003

Walks the full six-stage pipeline that your lab notebook implements: raw text, tokenization and stopword removal, TF-IDF vectorization, learned coefficients, the dot-product scoring step, and finally the sigmoid that converts a score into a probability and a label. When something goes wrong in the lab, it will usually be traceable to one stage. Keeping this picture in your head makes debugging much easier.

Discussion questions for class:
  1. A movie review says "not bad at all." After preprocessing and TF-IDF weighting, what does that review look like to the classifier? What information gets lost, and does it matter?
  2. The logistic regression model assigns a score to every word in the vocabulary. If "waste" has a large negative score, what did the model learn from the training data? What kind of reviews would have pushed that score negative?
  3. The model is trained on 1,600 labeled reviews and tested on 400 it has never seen. Why is it important that the vocabulary is built only from the training reviews, not from the test reviews?
  4. You are comparing TF-IDF + logistic regression to Gemini on the same 50 reviews. What does each approach "know" going in? Where would you expect the classical model to outperform Gemini, and where would you expect Gemini to win?
Bridge to Computer Vision (AIML 2013): CV students this week are computing HOG descriptors — explicit feature engineering that converts raw pixel data into a fixed-length numerical vector. TF-IDF and HOG solve the same problem in different domains: both discard irrelevant variation (common words, pixel noise), keep meaningful structure (distinctive terms, gradient orientations), and produce vectors that support cosine similarity comparison. In Week 4, both courses replace these hand-crafted vectors with learned representations: all-MiniLM-L6-v2 sentence embeddings for text, MobileNetV2 features for images.
