The Task

In the readings and in class, you learned that text must become numbers before any machine learning model can process it. This week you will see what that looks like in practice. You will build a sentiment classifier that decides whether a movie review is positive or negative twice: once using TF-IDF feature vectors and logistic regression, and once using the Gemini API with progressively stronger prompts.

The comparison is the point. TF-IDF turns text into a sparse vector by counting word frequencies. Gemini encodes meaning in a completely different way. Both can reach similar accuracy on this task, but they fail on different kinds of examples. Finding those differences tells you something important about what each approach actually understands.

Your Dataset

NLTK Movie Reviews

2,000 movie reviews (1,000 positive, 1,000 negative), built into NLTK. No download or account required. You will split it 80/20 into training and test sets. The TF-IDF vectorizer is fitted only on the training set. The Gemini classifier sees reviews one at a time and is never fitted on anything. Both are evaluated on the same held-out test set.

# Cell 3: Build TF-IDF feature vectors

What it does: Creates and fits a TF-IDF vectorizer on the training data only, then transforms both the training and test sets. Prints the matrix shape, sparsity percentage, and the top 10 TF-IDF terms for one sample positive review and one sample negative review.

Takes in: X_train, X_test, y_train, label_str (from Cell 2).

Must produce:

  • vectorizer. A TfidfVectorizer instance (from sklearn.feature_extraction.text) instantiated with parameters max_features=5000 (keep only the 5,000 most frequent terms), stop_words='english' (ignore common words like “the”, “and”), and ngram_range=(1, 2) (track individual words and pairs of consecutive words). Call .fit_transform(X_train) to learn the vocabulary and transform in one step.
  • X_train_tfidf: scipy sparse matrix, shape (1600, 5000). Result of vectorizer.fit_transform(X_train). Each row is one review; each column is one term (word or bigram). Cell values are TF-IDF scores (higher = term is distinctive for that review and corpus).
  • X_test_tfidf: scipy sparse matrix, shape (400, 5000). Result of vectorizer.transform(X_test). Critical: use .transform(), not .fit_transform(), to apply the same vocabulary learned from training data.
  • feature_names: numpy array of strings, length 5,000. The word/bigram vocabulary in feature-index order. Get from vectorizer.get_feature_names_out().
  • Printed output: matrix shapes, sparsity percentage, and top 10 highest TF-IDF terms for one positive and one negative sample review.

Notes: Sparsity (percentage of zero values) is computed as 1 - (nnz / (rows * cols)) where nnz is the number of non-zero values. To print top terms for a sample: find one positive review using next(i for i, l in enumerate(y_train) if l == 1) and one negative using l == 0. Convert that row to a dense array with .toarray()[0], then use np.argsort() to sort indices by score, take the last 10 (highest scores), reverse the order, and index into feature_names.
